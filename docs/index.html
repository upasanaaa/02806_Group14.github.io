SAN FRANCISCO UNDER WATCH

How Crime Patterns Transformed During the Pandemic Era

When the COVID-19 pandemic hit San Francisco on 17th of March in 2020, it changed the everyday life in visible ways. Offices closed, streets emptied, and most people stayed home. These changes were expected to affect public safety and crime but how, and to what? How did criminal behavior respond to the pandemic? Which neighborhoods saw drops in crimes, and which saw them rise? This story dives into crime data to answer that question.

About the Data: This analysis uses the San Francisco Police Department's incident report dataset, containing records of all reported crime incidents in San Francisco. The dataset includes information about crime categories, locations, dates, and times. For this analysis, we compare three distinct periods: before COVID (2018-2019), during lockdown (March 2020 - June 2021), and after restrictions eased (2022-2023).

Crime Trends During the Pandemic

The COVID-19 pandemic created a natural experiment for studying how crime patterns respond to major disruptions in urban life. Our analysis of six key crime categories reveals distinct responses to the lockdown measures.

Figure 1: Monthly incident counts for overall crime and five specific categories (Vehicle Theft, Vandalism, Burglary, Drug/Narcotic offenses, and Assault) from 2018-2022. Red vertical lines indicate the beginning (March 17, 2020) and end (June 15, 2021) of the lockdown period.

As shown in Figure 1, overall crime incidents dropped significantly when lockdown began, reflecting the sudden decrease in human activity across the city. However, not all crime categories responded the same way:

  • Vehicle Theft increased during lockdown, which could be expected with many cars sitting unused for extended periods, creating opportunities for thieves.
  • Burglary showed an initial spike during early lockdown which at first glance seems suprising as with more people staying inside one could expect that category to occur less frequently, however this is most likely suggesting that burglars targeted closed businesses and empty commercial properties.
  • Drug/Narcotic offenses decreased during lockdown, likely due to the fact that there was much less drug related crimes in public due to most of the people staying at home
  • Assault cases declined as expected with fewer people interacting in public spaces, and the number of incidents was going up gradually and corelated with the loosening of the restrictions.
  • Vandalism saw a moderate decline during early lockdown however the incident number wasn't much different compared to the previous years. After the official lockdown restrictions liftoff the incident number has spiked significantly.

By the time lockdown restrictions were lifted in June 2021, most crime categories were already trending back toward pre-pandemic levels, though with some notable differences in their recovery patterns. Vehicle theft for example stayed more or less on the same level as druing lockdown and drug related offences were not spiking a lot in number of occurences after the lockdown. Still by looking at the crime trend before, during and after lockdown it's can be said that Covid-19 pandemic has affected the SF criminals as much as any other human in the world.

Mapping Crime Across Lockdown

To better understand how the pandemic shaped the distribution of crime across San Francisco, the total number of reported incidents in each police district during three different time periods was mapped. Each map uses color intensity to show the number of incidents per district darker shades indicate more reported crimes. This approach helps highlight that crime is not just a matter of "how much," but also of "where."

During the lockdown period, we can observed a significant drop in crime in traditionally high-traffic districts such as Southern and Central, which encompass downtown, the Financial District, and parts of SOMA (South of Market). These are areas that would typically see high number of activity—commuters, tourists, nightlife. With fewer people on the streets, opportunities for certain types of crime likely decreased. These shifts are reflected clearly on the map. In contrast, more residential districts such as Ingleside, Bayview, and Taraval maintained or even saw increases in reported incidents. These neighborhoods are characterized by homes, schools, and local businesses—and with more people staying home during the pandemic, the dynamics of everyday life (and crime) shifted away from city centers and into residential areas.

In contrast, more residential districts such as Ingleside, Bayview, and Taraval increase in reported incidents. These neighborhoods are characterized by homes, schools, local businesses and with more people staying home during the pandemic, the dynamics of everyday life (and crime) shifted away from city centers and into residential areas.

After restrictions eased in mid 2021, we see a partial return of incidents to downtown areas, but not fully back to the levels before the pandemic. This may indicate a transformation in urban behavior, such as a rise in remote work, fewer daily commuters, or changes in how and where people report crime. These maps shows more than just numbers, they show how crime is linked to the people's life in the city. When their life changes for example due to a global pandemic so does the landscape of crime.

A recent article in the San Francisco Chronicle analyzed police data and came to similar conclusions: The reported crime dropped significantly in city-center districts during the pandemic and only partially returned to pre-COVID levels. They noted that some residential districts saw steady or rising incidents, which aligns with the patterns we see in our maps. Researchers also pointed to broader factors like remote work, economic shifts, and underreporting, all of which may have influenced the numbers.

Hourly Crime Patterns: What the Heatmap Reveals About The Crimes in the City

Beyond mapping where crime happens, we can explore other dimensions: the new question is What time. Did the timing of criminal activity shift as well? This question led to an interesting exploration of crime's temporal patterns across the city's divers neighborhoods.

Figure 3: Interactive heatmap showing crime occurrence by hour and category. Users can filter by district and pandemic period to explore patterns.

This heatmap shows the daily rhythm of San Francisco. Each column represents a crime category, while rows track the 24-hour cycle. The color intensity tells the story: darker cells reveal higher incident counts at that hour. The reader can select a district and preferred time period, uncovering patterns unique to the selected neighborhood during different time periods.

The heatmap shows that most crimes in San Francisco don't usually happen at specific time periods, they are rather evenly spread throught 24h. The only exception to it is porstitution. This pattern stayed the same across all pandemic periods, such as before, during, and after lockdowns. Even though daily life changed drastically during COVID-19 with people working from home, nightlife shutting down, and streets nearly empty, the times when crimes occurred remained surprisingly consistent.

This finding is important because it shows that crime's daily patterns don't change easily, even during major disruptions like a global pandemic. The heatmap helps visualize this clearly by showing how different types of crime vary by hour and by district. For public safety planners, police departments, and local authorities, this kind of insight is valuable. It helps them understand not just where and how much crime is happening but also when to expect it, allowing them to plan resources, patrols, and prevention strategies more effectively.

The data reveals that overall crime dropped sharply during the initial lockdown in March 2020, because of the public activity decreased. However not all crime categories followed the same trend. Vehicle theft rose, likely due to unattended cars, while drug offenses and assault declined. Mapping the data geographically showed how crime shifted from busy downtown districts to residential neighborhoods like Bayview, Ingleside, and Taraval, where more people were staying home. Even after restrictions eased in 2021, crime levels in the city center remained lower than before the pandemic, suggesting long-term behavioral shifts such as remote work. A dynamic heatmap reveals that the timing of crime remained stable. Most crimes don't follow a specific hourly pattern, but are spread throughout the day with one clear exception which is porstitution. This consistency underscores the importance of time-based policing strategies, even during large-scale societal disruptions. The story highlights the pandemic didn’t just shift the numbers, it affected SF's crime landscape.
