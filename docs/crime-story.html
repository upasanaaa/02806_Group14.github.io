🔍 Crime in San Francisco: The COVID-19 Lockdown Impact

A visual data story exploring how pandemic policies changed crime patterns in the city

Dataset: This story is based on the official San Francisco Crime dataset (2018–2022), containing timestamps, categories, and districts of reported incidents.

When San Francisco entered lockdown on March 17, 2020, its streets emptied and daily routines paused. But did crime stop too? This story unpacks the shifts in urban crime patterns caused by the pandemic — from the most dangerous hours of the day to which neighborhoods saw the biggest drops (or spikes).

🗺️ Which Districts Stayed Unsafe During Lockdown?

Even with strict movement restrictions, districts like Tenderloin and Southern continued reporting high crime. These areas, historically vulnerable due to social factors, didn’t quiet down like others. Meanwhile, residential zones like Richmond saw a sharp drop in activity.

📍 Before, During, and After Lockdown

Compare how crime shifted spatially over time. While lockdowns led to an overall dip, patterns varied district by district.

🔹 Before Lockdown

Pre-COVID crime was widespread, especially in commercial and nightlife hubs. Mobility meant opportunity for more incidents.

🔹 After Lockdown

Post-June 2021, crime rose again — not quite returning to pre-pandemic levels, but shifting toward property and vehicle crimes as city activity resumed.

🕒 What Time of Day Is Most Dangerous?

Even during lockdown, 6–9 PM remained the riskiest hours. People still ventured out in the evenings — and so did petty crime. Curfews and reduced nightlife lowered late-night incidents, but early evening still posed a threat.

📅 Weekday vs Weekend Crime

Weekends historically bring higher crime — nightlife, gatherings, and social events increase risk. During lockdown, this trend shrunk, but not evenly. Weekends still saw relatively more incidents in areas like Mission and Tenderloin.

🧠 Key Takeaways

  • Tenderloin remained the most high-crime district across all periods.
  • Safe zones included Park, Richmond, and Taraval.
  • Evenings (6–9 PM) were the most dangerous time of day.
  • Weekend crimes stayed slightly elevated, even in lockdown.
  • Crime changed — but it didn’t disappear.

🔚 Final Thoughts

The pandemic altered how and when crime occurred in San Francisco. While overall incidents fell, the resilience of some patterns highlights deep-rooted social and structural issues. Understanding these shifts can help design smarter urban policies in times of crisis and beyond.
